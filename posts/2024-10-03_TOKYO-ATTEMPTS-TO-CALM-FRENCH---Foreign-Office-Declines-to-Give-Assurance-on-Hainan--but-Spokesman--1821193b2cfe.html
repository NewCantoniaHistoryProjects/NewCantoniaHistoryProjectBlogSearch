TOKYO ATTEMPTS TO CALM FRENCH / Foreign Office Declines to Give Assurance on Hainan, but Spokesman Is Soothing (1939)

MENACE TO PEACE DENIED / Japanese Military Forces Take Over Customs at Haikow on Seized Isle

TOKYO, Monday, Feb. 13. — French Ambassador Arsene Henry visited Foreign Minister Hachiro Arita at 11:30 this morning in reference to the Japanese occupation of Hainan Island.

The Foreign Office declined to make any statement regarding the future of the island, but the spokesman’s remarks this morning seemed intended to allay foreign apprehensions without giving concrete assurances. He quoted Generalissimo Chiang Kai-shek’s remark that Japan’s occupation of Hainan was a menace to the peace of the Pacific.

“Japan’s occupation of Hainan,” said Tatsuo Kawai, chief of the information section, “has no connection with the peace of the Pacific.”

When asked if it was correct to say that Japan had declined to give any assurance regarding the future of Hainan, Mr. Kawai replied that it was premature to say any thing about the future, but added: “Our declaration that Japan has no territorial ambitions in China applies also to Hainan.”

The Japanese military forces have taken over the Haikow customs from Commissioner Goldsmith, an American of the Chinese Maritime Customs Service.

Mr. Goldsmith was formally notified and replied, according to Domei, Japanese press service, that he took no exception to the Japa- nese action. The transfer to the Japanese military authorities was scheduled for 2 P. M. today.

Vice Consul Matsudaira, from Hong Kong, with three consular officials, arrived at Haikow to take charge of diplomatic questions.

Figures on the Hainan seizure given out by the Japanese are that 230 Chinese were killed on Friday, while the Japanese reported three killed and two wounded.
